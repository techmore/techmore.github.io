Portable backup

Pecron E3800LFP starter plan.

A practical outage box for fridge, internet, lighting, phone charging, a standing fan, and the Jura coffee machine. Treat it as portable backup and small solar charging, not as the main HVAC or whole-home solar system.

Battery

3.84 kWh

LiFePO4 storage, about 3.2-3.5 kWh usable after AC inverter losses.

Output

4,200 W

Enough headroom for the Jura if heavy loads are managed.

Solar input

3,000 W

Two PV inputs, each 32-150V and 20A, up to 1,500W each.

Cycle rating

3,500+

Cycles to 80% capacity. Good for outages and moderate solar use.

Recommendation

Buy the unit first, then start with two panels.

The E3800LFP is strongest as a portable battery with enough inverter headroom to run normal outage loads. For solar, two 300W panels are the sane starter set: useful, movable, and not a huge yard project. Four panels are the better solar setup if you want meaningful daytime refill.

Minimum

Two 300W panels

This is the right first buy. It can help refill fridge/network/light usage during the day and stays portable enough to move around the yard.

Expected refill: about 1.5-2.5 kWh on a decent sun day.

Recommended

Four 300W panels

This is the practical sweet spot if solar charging matters. It gives the E3800LFP enough input to recover faster while still avoiding a fixed permit-scale installation.

Expected refill: about 3-5 kWh on a decent sun day.

Not first

Eight 300W panels

The E3800LFP can accept a lot of solar, but eight portable panels are bulky and expensive. If the goal grows this large, revisit fixed panels and EG4 planning.

Expected refill: strong, but awkward to deploy.

Load fit

What it should run

Load Fit Notes
Jura ENA Micro 9 Yes About 1,450W while heating/brewing. Avoid running other large loads at the same moment.
Fridge Yes Usually manageable. Compressor surge is why the larger inverter matters.
Network gear Yes Use a small UPS for graceful NAS/network shutdown and let Pecron be the long-duration source.
Lights, phones, fan Yes Good match. These are the loads that make the battery last.
Mitsubishi HVAC No plan Maybe possible in narrow 240V/light-load cases, but not a reliable or clean HVAC backup design.

Critical-loads subpanel

A small Square D panel could make this cleaner.

The useful version is not removing random circuits from the grid. It is moving a few chosen circuits into a small critical-loads subpanel, then feeding that subpanel through a code-compliant transfer setup so it can be powered by the grid normally or by the Pecron during an outage.

Good candidate circuits

Fridge

High value, moderate average load, startup surge needs inverter headroom.

Network / office

Gateway, switch, AP, NAS, and chargers if the circuit is cleanly scoped.

Selected lights

LED lighting gives a lot of comfort for very little battery draw.

Small kitchen outlet

Only if it is intentionally managed for the Jura and not shared with heavy appliances.

Component Purpose Planning note
Small Square D load center Holds the selected backup breakers. Size it for a few 120V circuits first. Do not include the 45A HVAC circuit in this Pecron plan.
Manual transfer switch or inlet/interlock Prevents grid backfeed and chooses grid or battery feed. This is the important safety part. The Pecron must never energize utility wiring accidentally.
Breaker and inlet matched to Pecron output Limits the subpanel to what the battery can safely supply. The breaker should be sized to the actual Pecron output and cable, not the old branch-circuit breaker size.
Circuit moves Moves only chosen loads from the main panel. Keep the list boring: fridge, network, a few lights, and maybe one managed outlet.

Recommendation

Yes, model a small Square D critical-loads subpanel, but keep it as an electrician-installed transfer system. It is a better fit than extension cords if you want repeatable outage behavior, and it is much cheaper and simpler than trying to make the Pecron act like the EG4 whole-home stack.

Wiring note

Keep panel voltage inside the input window.

The E3800LFP solar inputs want 32-150V. One PV300 panel is around 36V operating voltage and about 41V open-circuit voltage, so one panel per input works. Two panels in series per input also works. Do not build long series strings that can exceed 150V open-circuit voltage, especially in cold weather.

How many

My buy order

  1. Buy the E3800LFP if the $1,199 price is still available.
  2. Start with two PV300 panels.
  3. Add two more panels if you actually deploy them and want faster recharge.
  4. Stop before eight portable panels unless the portable setup is clearly working for you.

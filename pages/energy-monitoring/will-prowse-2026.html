Design decision

Build for selected 120V loads first.

Your Emporia data says the whole house averages about 60.5 kWh/day. Heat pump, water heater, and dryer are the wrong first targets for a cheap backup stack. Removing those loads leaves roughly 22 kWh/day for the circuits that actually fit this design: fridge, network, lights, office, phone charging, ERV controls, and occasional small kitchen use.

Install notes

What this does and does not solve.

Treat this as a blueprint for an electrician conversation. It is not a finished permitted design. The practical next step is choosing the exact backed-up circuits and deciding whether the 10kW split-phase path is worth the extra $910 over the 5kW micro path.

  • Include: refrigerator, network rack, lights, office outlets, phone charging, ERV controls, and limited kitchen outlet use.
  • Exclude: HVAC, water heater, dryer, EV charging, range, and other large 240V loads.
  • Coffee maker: fine as a short 120V load, but avoid stacking it with other kitchen loads during battery operation.
  • EG4 12000XP note: stronger ecosystem option at $1,899, but this page keeps it as a reference, not the priced build.
  • Required review: certification, inverter/battery communication, disconnects, fusing, conductor sizing, grounding, labeling, battery location, permits, and warranty support.

Grid charging

A 120V cord can charge, but it is slow.

Both inverter paths have AC charging behavior, but the safe setup is not an improvised extension cord into house wiring. Treat grid charging as either a dedicated inlet/breaker setup or a temporary bench-style charger connection sized by an electrician.

10kW split-phase model

Dedicated input preferred.

Listings/manual references show AC input support, including 120V single-phase ranges, but this belongs on a properly wired AC input circuit or inlet. Do not model it as a normal appliance cord unless the manual and electrician explicitly approve that wiring method.

Refill estimate: at a 12A continuous 120V source, expect roughly 17-20 hours after losses. A larger dedicated input circuit can be faster if the inverter settings and wiring support it.

5kW 120V micro model

Better cord-style candidate.

This is the more natural 120V charge candidate because it is a 120V output inverter charger and the listing calls out a 40A AC battery charger. A normal 15A outlet cannot feed the full charger rate.

Refill estimate: a 15A outlet should be treated around 12A continuous, so about 17-20 hours. If a dedicated 20A 120V circuit is allowed, estimate roughly 13-16 hours.

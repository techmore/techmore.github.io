A small, practical backup model for selected 120V house loads. The goal is to keep normal-life circuits running without paying for full-home HVAC, water-heater, dryer, or permitted whole-home solar infrastructure.

Design decision

Build for selected 120V loads first.

Your Emporia data says the whole house averages about 60.5 kWh/day. Heat pump, water heater, and dryer are the wrong first targets for a cheap backup stack. Removing those loads leaves roughly 22 kWh/day for the circuits that actually fit this design: fridge, network, lights, office, phone charging, ERV controls, and selected outlet use.

Solar selection

Model 20 panels as the practical target.

Six panels was too small for the measured load, and 14 panels still left too much daily shortfall. Twenty panels is the better target if the space, mounting, wiring, and budget work. Final series/parallel layout still needs panel Voc, cold-weather voltage, and inverter MPPT limits checked before purchase.

Install notes

What this does and does not solve.

Treat this as a blueprint for an electrician conversation. It is not a finished permitted design. The practical next step is choosing the exact backed-up circuits around the 10kW split-phase path and deciding whether to start with the new or refurbished inverter.

  • Include: refrigerator, network rack, lights, office outlets, phone charging, ERV controls, and selected outlet use.
  • Exclude: HVAC, water heater, dryer, EV charging, range, and other large 240V loads.
  • EG4 12000XP note: stronger ecosystem option at $1,899, but this page keeps it as a reference, not the priced build.
  • Required review: certification, inverter/battery communication, disconnects, fusing, conductor sizing, grounding, labeling, battery location, permits, and warranty support.

Grid charging

A 120V cord can charge, but it is slow.

The 10kW inverter has AC charging behavior, but the safe setup is not an improvised extension cord into house wiring. Treat grid charging as either a dedicated inlet/breaker setup or a temporary bench-style charger connection sized by an electrician.

10kW split-phase model

Dedicated input preferred.

Listings/manual references show AC input support, including 120V single-phase ranges, but this belongs on a properly wired AC input circuit or inlet. Do not model it as a normal appliance cord unless the manual and electrician explicitly approve that wiring method.

Refill estimate: at a 12A continuous 120V source, expect roughly 8-10 hours after losses. A larger dedicated input circuit can be faster if the inverter settings and wiring support it.

Plain-English readout

This is a critical-load battery system, not a heat-pump offset system.

The useful version is the 2-kit Callsun build. It gives enough solar to support the small daily loads and recover the battery on good days, but it does not make the Barn's heat pump, water heater, dryer, or electric cooking feel solar-backed.

Question Answer Why it matters
What should I expect? A selected-circuit backup panel for fridge/outlets, radon, internet, well pump, and basic lighting. Those loads average about 5.8 kWh/day in the Emporia model.
Is the 2-kit upgrade worth modeling? Yes. It moves the array from ~1.7-4.0 to ~3.4-8.0 kWh/day. That changes the system from "slows discharge" to "can recover on decent sun days."
Can it cover the heat pump? Maybe as a managed recent-weather stretch, not as guaranteed backup. May+June heat-pump use is ~7.65 kWh/day, but winter stress-test days are much larger.
Can it use the VEVOR charger? Not for this 48V battery bank. The VEVOR RV converter is a 12V charger; use inverter AC input or a proper 48V charger instead.
What about a 58.4V 20A charger? Possible as a rescue/top-off charger. It is about 1.17kW DC, so it is useful as a rescue charger when solar is weak and comfort loads matter.

Upgrade option

Mounting angle

Set the rack to match the season, or pick a fixed compromise.

For the Barn, a fixed array is simplest, but adjustable ground-mount hardware gives us a better seasonal fit. Signature Solar lists the IntegraRack IR-30 BallastRack as a fixed 30° frame at $129.99 per panel frame, and the IR EarthScrews 10-pack at $129.99 as the concrete-free mounting kit. Face panels true south for best performance in the Northern Hemisphere.

Higher-capacity battery note

The Yixiang EVE MB56 32 kWh finished battery is still the cleaner more-power option. Adding it back in place of the DIY kit and heater raises the model by about $1,579, but usable AC storage rises from about 11.6 kWh to about 23 kWh. That is the better match if overnight margin matters more than lowest entry price.

The updated model is 1 linked 900W panel pair plus the battery, inverter, a DC disconnect, and a proper load center for selected house circuits. Before buying, verify panel Voc, cold-weather voltage rise, MPPT voltage range, conductor sizing, fusing, and disconnect requirements. The inverter supports split-phase 120/240V output and AC/grid charging modes, so a night-charge or utility-top-off path is possible in principle, but it should be built as a transfer-input or charge-input design that cannot backfeed the grid.

Converter note

The VEVOR RV converter is not a fit for directly charging this modeled battery bank. It is a 110V AC to 12V DC RV converter/charger, while the Prowse stack is a 48V battery system. A 58.4V 20A LiFePO4 charger, like the BROODAY option, is a more plausible rescue/top-off charger because 58.4V matches a 16S LiFePO4 full-charge voltage. At 20A it is about 1.17kW DC, so expect roughly 10 hours to replace 11.6 kWh usable AC equivalent, or about 14 hours to refill a 16.1 kWh nominal battery before losses and taper. It still needs a fused DC connection, disconnect, BMS compatibility check, and a plan so it does not fight the inverter charger.

Compatibility check

What fits together, and what still needs verification.

Treat this as a blueprint for an electrician conversation. The broad architecture fits: PV into a 48V split-phase inverter, 48V battery on the DC side, and a critical-load panel on the AC output side. The exact wiring still needs a licensed review before buying because PV string voltage, transfer design, battery communication, and breaker assignments are where mistakes get expensive.

58.4V 20A LiFePO4 charger to battery bank Possible rescue option Confirm 16S LiFePO4 profile, BMS charge-current limits, AC circuit capacity, fusing, connector, disconnect, and whether the inverter's AC input charger is cleaner.
Heat pump to backed-up panel Not this build Model separately as a larger solar/storage project or controlled load-shed/comfort strategy.

Panel count

Two panel pairs is the better solar version.

If the goal is to support more daytime load, keep the same inverter and battery stack but double the panels. That does not turn this into a whole-house solar offset system, but it materially improves recharge speed, cloudy-day resilience, and the chance of supporting the well or HVAC-adjacent 120V loads while other circuits stay on.

System map

This is the wiring story in one view.

The cleaner MVP is PV into the inverter, inverter into a proper load center, and then breakers for the critical circuits. If we later want grid-top-off or utility assist, the first-choice path is the inverter's AC input or a licensed transfer design. A separate 58.4V LiFePO4 charger can be a rescue battery top-off path for poor solar windows, but it is not the main power path.

System output

What this system actually gives you.

The updated model is the 900W bifacial pair you sent: two 450W panels, about 2.4 kWh/day annual average in Tinicum, and a much smaller but realistic off-grid test path. If we double the panel count to two 900W pairs, the solar side rises to about 1.8 kW DC and roughly 4.8 kWh/day average. With no solar contribution, the DIY battery gives about 11.6 kWh usable AC, or roughly a full day on the narrow priority load set.

Mounting angle

Set the rack to match the season, or pick a fixed compromise.

For the Barn, a fixed array is simplest, but adjustable ground-mount hardware gives us a better seasonal fit. Current Connected lists the IntegraRack IR-45ASA adjustable frame at about $199.99 and the IR-15/IR-30 fixed-angle frame at $129.99. Face panels true south for best performance in the Northern Hemisphere.

Install notes

What this does and does not solve.

Treat this as a blueprint for an electrician conversation. It is not a finished permitted design. The practical next step is choosing the exact backed-up circuits around the 10kW split-phase path and deciding whether to start with the new or refurbished inverter.

Electrician checklist

Confirm the selected-load panel, inverter certification, battery communication, disconnects, fusing, conductor sizing, grounding, labeling, battery location, permits, and warranty support before treating this as purchase-ready.

Panel count

Two panel pairs is the better solar version.

If the goal is to support more daytime load, keep the same inverter and battery stack but double the panels. That does not turn this into a whole-house solar offset system, but it materially improves recharge speed, cloudy-day resilience, and the chance of supporting the well or HVAC-adjacent 120V loads while other circuits stay on.

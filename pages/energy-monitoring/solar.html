Tinicum Permit Reality Check

Ground solar is not just an electrical project. Tinicum lists solar on the ground as requiring both a Zoning Permit Application and Building Permit Application. Roof solar is listed as requiring the building permit path.

Permit cost estimate: the 19.8 kW array falls in the township alternative-energy 11-100 kW tier. Using the published $22/kW fee gives $435.60. Add the current $100 application review fee and the listed $25 electrical processing fee, so the basic township permit placeholder is $560.60+. Third-party electrical plan review/inspection is separate and paid by the applicant.

Scale matters. Six panels may feel minor, but the township still lists solar as a permit item. The full 36-panel pallet is about 999 sq ft of panel surface before row spacing, access, trenching, and equipment pads, so it is much harder to treat as informal or visually incidental.

Likely review items

  • Scaled site plan, setbacks, lot boundaries, well/septic, slopes, wetlands, woods, and overlays.
  • Electrical permit, third-party PA electrical review, rough/final electrical inspections.
  • Ground-mount height, building envelope, 110% property-energy sizing limit, and historic-resource approval if applicable.
  • Stormwater/grading review if disturbance, trenching, access, or equipment pads trigger it.

ZNShine Panel Fit

Pallet output

19.8 kW DC

36 panels × 550 W.

Panel footprint

~999 sq ft

Each panel is about 7.48 ft × 3.72 ft, or 27.8 sq ft before row spacing/service access.

Best string shape

4 × 9

Four strings of nine panels: two strings on MPPT 1 and two strings on MPPT 2.

Avoid

10/string

Ten in series risks high cold-weather voltage against FlexBOSS protection limits.

Compatibility read: ZNShine lists 50.20 V Voc, 41.90 V Vmp, 13.89 A Isc, and 13.13 A Imp. A 9-panel string is about 452 V Voc and 377 V Vmp at STC, which fits the FlexBOSS21 voltage window. Two parallel strings are near the MPPT current limit, so final stringing should be checked by the installer, especially if bifacial rear gain will be high.

Bifacial ground-mount note: bifacial gain is most useful when the back of the panel sees reflected light. A raised ground mount over light gravel, concrete, or snow-friendly open ground should do better than a tight roof mount. Treat that as upside, not base output, because ground surface, row spacing, tilt, and height drive the real gain.

How Panels Stack Up

For the inverter: no. 19.8 kW DC is under the FlexBOSS21 recommended 21 kW STC array power. Bifacial rear gain may cause clipping, but clipping is different from incompatibility.

For two batteries: much better. A 32 kWh-class battery bank can absorb more midday surplus and carry more load into the evening, though it still is not sized for full winter HVAC backup.

Best next move: keep the full array plan if mounting works, include two batteries in the first financial model, and verify mount/BOS cost before ordering.

Minimal install option: mount 18 panels first as two 9-panel strings, keep the other 18 for expansion, and wire the design so adding the second half is straightforward.

Why The Water Heater Still Matters

The HVAC heat pump is now the big load. Solar planning has to preserve heating/cooling comfort first, because the heat pump is 44.0% of measured mains and sits on a 45 A / 240 V circuit.

The water heater is the clean controllable load. It is 18.1% of measured mains, and an 80 gallon HPWH can reduce baseline demand while also being scheduled into solar hours.

Priority: keep grid fallback for HVAC, reduce water-heater load, then use EG4 solar to offset daytime heat-pump and household use.

Decision Rule

Buy EG4 first only as a complete stage if the goal is barn/home offset, no intentional export, and using Emporia data to tune the system over time.

Use the full pallet if mounting works. The array is large, but it fits the FlexBOSS21 better than a tiny starter array would.

Model battery #2 now. More storage improves capture, evening use, and cloudy-day usefulness, so the second battery belongs in the pricing model.

Step 1

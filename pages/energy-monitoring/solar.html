System definition

What Phase 1 is trying to do

Phase 1 is a permitted, grid-backed solar and battery system sized around the measured house loads. It should prioritize HVAC offset, water heating, daytime household use, and evening carryover. It should not be presented as a promise to run winter heat overnight from batteries.

Rack reality

The 30-panel plus 10-panel Chiko model is expensive, but it gives a permit-friendly high-wind mounting basis. Wood may be cheaper, but should not be assumed wind-resistant, bondable, or permit-friendly without engineered details.

Tax credit note

Model assumes $0 federal credit unless verified by a tax professional. If any credit applies, treat it as a one-time tax benefit or carryforward, not recurring bill savings.

Permitting

Tinicum Township review path

A six-panel experiment may look minor in the yard, but Tinicum lists solar as a permit item. A 36-panel ground array is about 999 square feet of module surface before row spacing, access paths, trenching, and equipment pads, so it should be treated as a real site-plan project.

  • Stormwater or grading review if trenching, equipment pads, access work, or disturbance triggers it.

Technical fit

Panel, inverter, and battery fit

The panel pallet fits the FlexBOSS21 on paper when arranged conservatively. The installer still needs to verify cold-weather voltage, parallel current, grounding, breaker placement, and the final one-line diagram.

  • Best string shape: four strings of nine panels, with two strings on MPPT 1 and two strings on MPPT 2.
  • Why not ten per string: ten-panel strings create more cold-weather high-voltage risk against protection limits.
  • Voltage read: nine panels are about 452V Voc and 377V Vmp at STC.
  • Array sizing: 19.8 kW DC is under the FlexBOSS21 recommended 21 kW STC array power; rear-side bifacial gain may clip at times.
  • Battery read: two batteries make the pallet more usable by storing more midday surplus, but they do not turn winter HVAC into an overnight battery load.

Panel size

~999 sq ft

Bifacial upside

Ground mounts help

Rear gain is better with air gap and reflective ground such as gravel, concrete, snow, or open light surfaces.

Starter option

18 panels first

Two nine-panel strings can reduce first-stage site work while keeping the rest of the pallet for expansion.

Load strategy

Use solar where it matters most

HVAC is the major energy user now, so the design should help with peak summer and winter bills without depending on batteries for full comfort backup. In shoulder months, the value comes from scheduling flexible loads into solar hours.

  1. 1. HVAC first: let solar directly offset cooling and heat-pump runtime when available.
  2. 2. Water heater next: current water-heater load is about 4,006 kWh/year, or $551/year at $0.13760/kWh.
  3. 3. Shift flexible loads: run laundry, charging, printing, and other noncritical work while solar is producing.
  4. 4. Keep grid fallback: treat the batteries as offset and resilience, not as a replacement for utility service.

Heat-pump water heater note

An 80-gallon heat-pump water heater is a load-reduction candidate, not a prerequisite for Phase 1 solar. If it drops annual water-heater use to roughly 1,225-2,000 kWh/year, the savings range is about $276-$383/year. That is usually a 6-10 year unit-only payback, or 9-22 years installed before rebates, so the solar scope should not depend on it.

Planning takeaway

The close blueprint

Phase 1 is strongest as a permitted 19.8 kW DC ground-mounted array with two 16 kWh batteries, FlexBOSS21, GridBOSS, proper breakers, proper BOS, and an honest permit/site-work allowance. The decision point is not whether the panels are useful; it is whether the mount, permit, labor, and site constraints make the full pallet worth doing now versus staging the array in smaller nine-panel string blocks.

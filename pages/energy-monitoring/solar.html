Recommended Phase 5 Direction

The best first move is still the EG4 deep-dive plan: FlexBOSS21 + GridBOSS, one 16 kWh wall battery, and roughly 9-12 kW DC of starter PV. That is enough solar to matter against the measured 60.5 kWh/day average without pretending one battery can cover extreme winter heating days.

The point is not full independence on day one. The point is reliable daytime offset, automatic grid fallback, and a clean base that can add battery #2 or more PV after you see actual production and curtailment.

One 16 kWh battery first. Grid stays connected for heat-pump headroom. Zero/no intentional export.

Pecron E3800LFP Read

What it is good for: portable backup, trailer/RV loads, temporary critical circuits through extension-cord style use, and charging from portable solar when the main solar project is not installed yet.

Why it is interesting: Pecron lists 3,840 Wh capacity, 4,200 W AC output, 12 V / 30 A DC output, 3,000 W max solar input through XT60-M, and expansion up to 26,880 Wh with EP3800-48V batteries.

Why it does not replace EG4: it does not solve whole-service no-backfeed integration by itself. It is a portable power station, while FlexBOSS/GridBOSS is the better architecture for controlled home solar offset.

Decision Rule

Buy EG4 first if the goal is barn/home offset, no intentional export, and using Emporia data to tune the system over time.

Consider Pecron if you need a near-term portable backup unit, want 12 V / 30 A DC for field loads, or want a movable battery before committing to permanent electrical work.

Do not oversize day one: one EG4 battery plus 9-12 kW PV is enough to learn. Add battery #2 before chasing full winter independence.

Step 1

Install control stack

GridBOSS + FlexBOSS21 creates the service/load integration point and automatic grid support.

Step 2

Start with one battery

The first 16 kWh battery buffers solar and transitions. It is not sized for the 178.7 kWh winter peak day.

Step 3

Add PV, then measure

Start at 9-12 kW DC, validate self-consumption and curtailment, then decide whether battery #2 or more PV is next.

Notes And Assumptions

  • Emporia source period: January 14, 2026 through May 20, 2026, with 7,681.8 kWh measured over 127 complete days.
  • Offset math uses $0.13760/kWh, 4 peak-sun-hours/day, and 85% system yield for planning only.
  • Heat pump is 44.0% of measured mains; water heater is 18.1%. Water heating is the cleaner load to shift or reduce first.
  • Heat pump breaker appears to be double-pole 45 A. At 240 V that is about 10.8 kVA of breaker capacity, but breaker size is not the same as typical operating draw.
  • Federal tax credit treatment depends on eligibility and tax liability; this page treats it as planning context, not guaranteed cash.
  • Final solar sizing still needs roof/ground-mount layout, shading, utility rules, permitting, and installer or electrician review.

Selected-circuit backup

Anker F3800 Plus entry plan.

A cleaner home-backup entry than the Pecron subpanel path, but not cheaper. At roughly $4k, it is about 3.3x the $1,199 Pecron base-unit price while still landing around 12-15% of the full EG4 project cost.

Battery

3.84 kWh

Same base battery size class as the Pecron E3800LFP.

Output

6,000 W

120/240V output with L14-30 support for critical-circuit backup.

Solar input

3,200 W

Two inputs, up to 1,600W each, 11-165V and 17A max.

Role

Entry

Useful backup appliance, not a full home energy system. Anker's cycle-life claim matters because the stack is compact and expensive per kWh.

Recommendation

This is the easy middle path, not the cheap path.

The Anker kit makes sense if the goal is critical-circuit backup with an app, 240V output, a supported home panel path, and less custom electrical design. It does not beat Pecron on price. Use the Anker option only if the cleaner home-integration path is worth paying several times more than the Pecron base unit.

HVAC runtime model

The recent HVAC data makes the battery requirement clear.

The Emporia export showed full-day HVAC usage around 34.9-45.1 kWh, with observed hourly peaks under 3kW. Anker output can cover that observed draw, but the battery stack needed for 20 hours is large.

20-hour answer

Battery-only, one Anker stack does not cleanly hit 20 hours for the recent full-day HVAC loads. To reach 20 hours at the observed 1.45-1.88 kW average draw, the system needs about 29-38 kWh usable. Maxed Anker expansion is still short without solar contribution. With strong daytime solar, the maxed stack may become workable for mild/moderate HVAC days, but this is no longer a cheap entry system.

Mounted solar

Emporia per-circuit diagnostics

Single kit versus double kit.

Anker is the cleanest integrated backup path here, so the useful question is how much solar support it gets. One kit keeps the system easy; two kits make the critical-load backup much more capable.

Build Solar / day Critical fit Readout
1 kit / 2 panels ~1.8-3.2 kWh/day Good for light critical circuits and app-managed backup. Easy entry.
2 kits / 4 panels ~3.6-6.4 kWh/day Much better at holding a critical-circuit load center through the day. Better if we want real daytime support.

Bottom line

Anker is the easiest home-backup entry, Pecron is the cheapest backup energy, and EG4 is the serious solar architecture. At $4k, Anker is over 3x the $1,199 Pecron base-unit price. The argument for Anker is not value per kWh; it is a cleaner critical-circuit ecosystem at a much lower entry cost than EG4.

Cutting My Electric Bill by $700/Year

Power costs, supplier switching, and assistance programs, March 2026

After getting a handle on all my bills, I decided to dig into my Met-Ed electric bills from the past year. What I found was shocking — I was significantly overpaying for electricity due to a bad supplier contract. Here's exactly what I did to cut my costs.

🚨 Priority #1: Ditch the Expensive Supplier

I was using AEP Energy as my electric generation supplier at 14.49 cents/KWH. But Met-Ed's "Price to Compare" (their default service rate) was only 11.04 cents/KWH.

The Math: Over 11 months, I used ~20,000 KWH. At 3.5 cents difference, that's roughly $650–$700 in pure overpayment just for choosing the wrong supplier.

What I did: Switched from AEP Energy (14.49¢) to Townsquare Energy (11.17¢) — a savings of about 3.3 cents per kWh. That's roughly $15/month or $180/year in pure savings, just by shopping around.

🔌 Official PA Comparison Tools

PAPowerSwitch — Pennsylvania's official electricity comparison site, run by the PUC. Enter your ZIP code to compare electric suppliers, rates, and plan terms. It's the go-to checker for PA's deregulated energy market.

PAGasSwitch — The companion site for natural gas rate comparisons in Pennsylvania.

Visit PAPowerSwitch → Visit PAGasSwitch →

💸 Priority #2: Cancel Junk Charges

Every single bill included a $6.99/month HomeServe charge — that's $84/year for an optional home repair protection plan I never used.

What I did: Called 888-658-3800 to cancel. Took about 5 minutes. Immediate $84/year savings.

Quick win: Review your bills for recurring charges you don't use — warranty programs, insurance add-ons, etc. Call to cancel.

📊 Priority #3: Equal Payment Plan

Switched to Met-Ed's Equal Payment Plan to lock in $135/month instead of wild swings from $300 in summer to $692 in winter.

How it works: They take a running 12-month average of your costs, lock you in at that amount each month, then do a "true-up" push or pull at the end of the year to settle up.

Note: Since I just got a heat pump system installed, my winter usage will likely increase this year. I should expect an extra $100-200/month this coming winter and budget accordingly.

🔍 What I Learned About My Usage

My usage pattern tells a clear story:

  • Summer: ~900–1,900 KWH/month
  • Winter: ~2,100–3,100 KWH/month

This dramatic winter jump strongly suggests electric heat (baseboard or heat pump). The good news: a new heat pump is typically 3x more efficient than electric baseboard, so my costs should normalize over time.

🆘 Assistance Programs to Look Into

If you qualify, these PA programs can help significantly:

LIHEAP

$200–$1,000 one-time grant sent directly to your utility. Based on income (150% federal poverty). Apply at compass.state.pa.us

PCAP

Met-Ed's Customer Assistance Program — percentage-of-income payment plan for low-income customers. Call 1-800-545-7741

LIURP/WARM

Free weatherization — insulation, sealing, heating system upgrades. Based on income and usage. Call 1-800-764-0111

Customer Service

Always worth a call — Met-Ed: 1-800-545-7741. Ask about budget billing, assistance programs, and payment plans.

What to expect for septic service, March 2026

Owning a home with a septic system means planning for regular maintenance. Here's what I learned about septic pumping costs and what to expect as a homeowner.

Net cost after coupon: $344.53 for pumping a 1000 gallon septic tank.

💡 Tip: Search for septic pumping coupons! I found LOVEYOURSEPTIC75 which saved $75. Many companies run seasonal promotions — it's worth checking before you book.

📅 How Often to Pump

General guidelines for septic pumping frequency:

  • Every 2-3 years for households with 1-2 people
  • Every 3-5 years for households with 3-4 people
  • Every 1-2 years if you have a garbage disposal (adds solids)
  • Every 1-2 years for heavy water users

🔧 Maintenance Tips

✅ Do

  • • Pump every 3-5 years (regular maintenance)
  • • Use septic-safe toilet paper
  • • Divert roof drains and surface water away from the drainfield
  • • Inspect the tank annually

❌ Don't

  • • Flush wipes, paper towels, or grease
  • • Use harsh chemical drain cleaners
  • • Park vehicles on the drainfield
  • • Plant trees near the septic system (roots!)

💵 Cost Breakdown

Average pump cost (1000 gal) $350–$500
Inspection $100–$250
Repair (if needed) $500–$3,000+
Annual maintenance budget ~$150–$200

Pro tip: Set aside $150-200/year for septic maintenance. It's cheaper than a emergency pump or repair. And always — always — use a coupon if you can find one!

Good news: Most standby generators run on natural gas or propane — which includes biogas! You'll just need to ensure proper fuel pressure and filtration.

🔧 Can You Run a Generator on Biogas?

Yes — with these requirements:

  • • Biogas must be "scrubbed" (H2S removed)
  • • Consistent gas pressure (typically 6-14" WC)
  • • May need gas compressor for steady flow
  • • Most natural gas generators can adapt

Biogas Filtering (H2S Removal):

  • • Iron oxide filters (most common)
  • • Activated carbon filters
  • • Water scrubbers
  • • Cost: $100-500 depending on size

DIY Biogas System

  • • Two IBC totes (~300 gal each)
  • • PVC piping & valves
  • • H2S filter
  • • Gas storage (bladder or dome)
  • Cost: ~$500-1,000

Note: Home biogas produces ~50-70% methane — lower BTU than natural gas. You'd need a large system or compressor to run a generator reliably. Mostly practical for cooking rather than heavy generator use.

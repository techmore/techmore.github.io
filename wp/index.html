1. Death Has Been Letting You Win July 9, 2017

To extend your life, you've played Death in many games and beaten him. However, after your last game, you begin to see that Death has been losing to you on purpose.

by I Hate Macish, July 9, 2017

The first time I beat Death, I was twelve years old. It was a simple game — chess, if you can believe it. The Reaper himself, manifestation of the inevitable end of all things, sat across from me in my grandmother's kitchen, looking for all the world like a tired old man in a rumpled suit.

I'd drawn the black pieces. Death had white. "You move first," he said, his voice like wind through dry leaves. "And choose wisely. This game determines more than you'd think."

I didn't understand then. I just knew that I couldn't let my grandmother die — she'd promised to take me to Disney World if I got good grades, and she was the only one who'd ever made me such promises.

I won in seventeen moves. Death smiled, a strange sad smile that didn't reach his hollow eyes, and faded like morning mist. Grandma lived another six years.

I didn't understand what I'd done. Not really. Not until I was twenty-three, sitting across from Death in a hospital room, my wife's hand going cold in mine.

"We meet again," Death said. His voice hadn't changed at all. Still that dry, autumnal whisper. "You know the rules. One game. Her life, or..."

I didn't let him finish. I pulled out my phone, set up a chess app. "One game," I said.

It took me thirty-two moves this time. I'd gotten better over the years. Learned strategy. Death played like a man who was tired, making mistakes a novice wouldn't make.

"You win again," Death said. He didn't sound surprised. He didn't sound upset. He sounded... relieved? "She has six more years. Use them well."

I should have noticed it then. The way he said "six years" — not a question, not a gamble, but a certainty. Like he knew exactly how the game would end. Like he was reading from a script.

But I was too busy being grateful. Too busy holding my wife's hand and crying into her shoulder while the monitors sang their song of continued life.

The years passed. Death and I played seven more times. My mother, my father, my best friend from college, two coworkers I cared about more than I admitted, and finally — finally — my wife, back for the second time.

Each game, I won. Each game, Death played a little more strangely. Opening moves that made no sense. Entire flanks left undefended. It was like watching a grandmaster intentionally throw a tournament game.

And then, last Tuesday, I finally understood.

Death came to me in my sleep. Not as the old man in the rumpled suit, but as something older. Something vast and tired and infinitely sad.

"You've beaten me forty-seven times," he said. "Forty-seven souls who should have passed on. Forty-seven threads I was forced to leave hanging in the tapestry of existence."

"Why?" I asked. "Why let me win?"

Death laughed — a sound like grave dirt shifting, like the last breath leaving a dying man's lungs.

"Because I'm tired," he said. "Because I've been doing this since before your species crawled from the primordial ooze. Because every soul I collect carries a little piece of my burden, and I've been carrying too much for too long."

"You could have just... stopped."

"Stopped?" Death's form flickered, and for a moment I saw what he really was — not a skeleton in a robe, not a monster, but a person. A man who'd been doing an impossible job for eternity. "There's no stopping. There's only passing the mantle to someone else. And I thought..."

"You thought?"

"I thought maybe you'd understand. Maybe you'd see what I've seen. Maybe you'd take some of the weight."

I woke up in a cold sweat. The sun was coming up. My wife was still sleeping next to me, alive, thanks to a game I'd won six years ago.

But now I understood. Death hadn't been losing to me. He'd been giving me a gift. An impossible gift — the lives of everyone I'd ever loved.

And now he was asking for something in return.

I don't know what he wants. I don't know what it would mean to take his place. But when I look in the mirror now, I see something different in my eyes.

Something old. Something tired. Something that's been playing chess against the Reaper for thirty years and finally, finally understands the game.

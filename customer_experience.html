Customer Experience

 Hospitality & Guest Experience

A curated reading list on hospitality, guest experience, and building customer loyalty. These books form the foundation for understanding how to create memorable experiences that turn one-time visitors into lifelong customers.

Be Our Guest

Amazon ↗

Disney Institute

Disney's proprietary approach to guest experience, focusing on exceeding expectations through attention to detail, cast member training, and creating magical moments. The foundation for many modern hospitality frameworks.

Why it matters

Disney basically invented systematic hospitality. Their "cast member" philosophy treats every employee as part of the show, and their attention to the entire guest journey—from parking to exit—sets the standard for experience design.

Setting the Table

Amazon ↗

Danny Meyer (2006)

The restaurateur bible. Meyer (Union Square Cafe, Shake Shack, etc.) breaks down hospitality as emotional connection + constant anticipation of what guests want before they ask. He shares hard numbers on repeat business and how one bad experience kills retention.

Key insight

Enlightened Hospitality—his philosophy that extends beyond customers to include employees and suppliers. When you take care of your people, they take care of customers. The stats on repeat business are compelling: small improvements in retention can double profits.

Unreasonable Hospitality

Amazon ↗

Will Guidara (2022)

Written by Danny Meyer's former partner (Eleven Madison Park). It's all about elevating "good" service to unforgettable experiences through anticipation, tiny surprises, and obsessive consistency. Tons of restaurant examples on how to make every guest feel like a regular from visit #1.

The play

Surprise and delight done right—not as a gimmick but as a systematic approach. The book is full of specific stories: bringing snow to a family who'd never seen it, creating a beach in a private dining room. These aren't accidents—they're built into culture.

The Experience Economy

Amazon ↗

B. Joseph Pine II & James H. Gilmore (1999, updated 2011)

The book that basically invented the idea that experiences beat products/services for loyalty. Uses Disney and restaurant examples to show how staging memorable moments creates emotional retention. Explains why the first visit is usually a loss-leader and how anticipation + consistency flips customers into regulars.

The framework

Four stages of value: commodities → goods → services → experiences. The money is in staging experiences that customers remember. The first visit builds anticipation, the visit itself creates the experience, and post-visit memory is what drives loyalty.

Delivering Happiness

Amazon ↗

Tony Hsieh (2010)

Zappos story, but the principles crush it for restaurants too. Hsieh obsessed over "wow" moments and culture that guarantees consistency no matter who's on shift. Heavy on long-term retention math (customer lifetime value) and how word-of-mouth acquisition explodes once people hit that "regular" threshold.

The culture play

Culture eats strategy for breakfast. Hsieh built Zappos around delivering happiness—for employees first, then customers. The "wow" moments are organic but the culture that enables them is systematic. Heavy on CLV (customer lifetime value) math.

The E-Myth Revisited

Amazon ↗

Michael E. Gerber (1986/2004)

Not a "customer service" book on the surface, but the one that nails process and consistency for small businesses like restaurants. Gerber shows how to build systems so the experience is identical every single visit—which is exactly what turns someone who came 3 times into a lifelong regular.

The systems play

Working ON your business, not IN it. The "franchise prototype" concept: document your processes so they can be replicated perfectly. Consistency across visits is what builds habit—which is what turns occasional visitors into regulars.

Raving Fans

Amazon ↗

Ken Blanchard & Sheldon Bowles (1993)

Short, story-driven, and laser-focused on going from "satisfied" to "raving" customers. It walks through the exact steps of meeting expectations, then exceeding them consistently, with real examples of retention payoff.

The framework

Three steps: 1) Discover what your customer wants, 2) Deliver exactly that (and communicate it), 3) Deliver even more. Simple but effective. The "customer comment card" concept from this book is everywhere now.

Customers for Life

Amazon ↗

Carl Sewell & Paul B. Brown (1990/2005)

Data-heavy look at lifetime customer value and retention (originally car dealerships but 100% applies to restaurants). Sewell tracked every stat imaginable—repeat rates, referral math, cost of losing someone after visit 1 vs. visit 4.

The data

The "need past 3 visits" insight—customers typically need 3+ visits before they become habitual. The cost of acquiring a new customer is 5x the cost of keeping an existing one. Detailed systems for anticipation and flawless execution.
